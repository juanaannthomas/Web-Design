About Kanjirappally

Kanjirappally is a beautiful town located in the eastern part of Kottayam district in Kerala, India. It lies on the foothills of the Western Ghats and is known for its scenic landscapes, fertile land, and cultural diversity. The town is also famous for its role as a connecting hub between the midlands and the high ranges of Kerala.

It is often referred to as the "Gateway to Highrange" because of its location on the route to popular hill stations like Thekkady, Vagamon, and Kuttikkanam. Agriculture, especially rubber plantations, plays a major role in the local economy. The town is also known for its educational institutions, religious harmony, and historical significance.

Town Highlights

  • Location: Kottayam District, Kerala
  • Known for: Rubber plantations, churches, cool climate, and connectivity to hill stations
  • Nearby Places: Erumeli, Manimala, Mundakayam, Vagamon, Thekkady
  • Transport: Well-connected by road; nearest railway station is Kottayam (44 km away)

Climate

Kanjirappally has a pleasant tropical monsoon climate. The area receives one of the highest amounts of rainfall in Kerala, thanks to the Southwest monsoon winds that rise up the Western Ghats. The abundant rainfall supports lush greenery and agriculture.

  • Summer (March to May): Warm and humid, with temperatures ranging from 25°C to 35°C
  • Monsoon (June to September): Heavy rainfall, very green and misty
  • Winter (November to February): Cool and pleasant, with temperatures between 20°C and 28°C

Main Attractions

Vagamon Hills

Vagamon Hills – A peaceful hill station with tea plantations, pine forests, and viewpoints.

Vagamon is an Indian hill station and a revenue village located in Peerumedu Taluk of Idukki district, and also Meenachil taluk and Kanjirappally taluk of Kottayam district in the state of Kerala, India.Located in the Western Ghats 25 kilometres (16 mi) east of Erattupetta on the border of Kottayam-Idukki districts, Vagamon is known for its natural environment. The Vagamon glass bridge is the longest cantilever glass bridge in India

St. Dominic's Cathedral

St. Dominic’s Cathedral – One of the largest and oldest churches in the region.

A brief history of Kanjirappally Pazhayapally (St.Mary’s Church) and the Puthenpally (St.Dominic’s Cathedral). The Old Church (St.Mary’s Church) and the market nearby have been constructed on a piece of land donated by the erstwhile King of Thekkemcore Sri. Veerakerala Perumal who was ruling the eastern areas including Kanjirappally on the 21st of Medam in the year K.V.624. (K V is for Malayalam year Kolla Varsham). On this plot the location of the Church building was decided by the King himself by planting a green stick on the chosen spot. So for those Christains who migrated from Nilakkal 100 years ago under the leadership of an elderly man named Thommi, who was also the trustee of the Nilakkal curch,got a church for the first time. They had to leave Nilakkal due to attack of burglars, spread of deadly virus diseases and wild insects called Vakri puli perumpatta. Until the new church was consecrated those people were going to the curch in Aruvithura (Erattupetta) for worship and their spiritual needs.

Rubber Plantations

Rubber Plantations – Endless stretches of green rubber estates across the region.

The well-distributed rainfall pattern of Kanjirappally is the primary reason for the phenomenon of high yield of latex from Rubber plantations in and around the town.

Culture & Traditions

Kanjirappally has a rich cultural heritage and is home to people of different religions living in harmony. Major festivals include Onam, Christmas, Vishu, Easter, Eid, and local church feasts. The town also has a strong Christian legacy with several historic churches, along with temples and mosques.

Kanjirappally, a picturesque town in Kerala's Kottayam district, is known for its rich cultural heritage, religious diversity, and vibrant traditions. Nestled amidst lush greenery and rubber plantations, the town is home to a harmonious blend of Christian, Hindu, and Muslim communities, each contributing to its unique cultural identity. Christianity, especially the Syrian Catholic tradition, has a strong presence in Kanjirappally, with historical churches like St. Dominic’s Cathedral standing as symbols of deep-rooted faith. Religious feasts, particularly those dedicated to saints, are celebrated with grandeur, featuring colorful processions, traditional music, and fireworks. The Hindu population also upholds age-old customs through temple festivals, where rituals, elephant processions, and classical performances like Kathakali add to the festive spirit. Muslims in Kanjirappally observe traditional practices with devotion, celebrating Eid and other religious events with prayers, communal meals, and acts of charity. Folk arts such as Margamkali, Parichamuttukali, and Duff Muttu reflect the region’s diverse cultural expressions, often showcased during festivals and social gatherings. As an agriculturally rich area, the people of Kanjirappally have a deep connection to the land, with farming traditions and harvest festivals forming an integral part of local life. The town is also known for its strong family values, communal harmony, and a shared respect for tradition across all communities, making it a vibrant and culturally rich part of Kerala.
